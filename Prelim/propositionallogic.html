Propositional Logic¶

We can turn out statements about set theory into statements about propositions, in the classical logic sense of TRUE and FALSE - so called Propositional Logic. If you’re interested in areas of logic where some of these ideas are challened, have a look at The Logic of Quantum Mechanics, by Birkhoff & Von Neumann.

A propositional or statement is a declaration that is either TRUE (T) or FALSE (F) but not both. Some propositions are composite or compound - i.e. composed of sub-propositions and then are connected in different ways. Others are primitive or unitary, i.e. cannot be broken down in to simpler propositions. One way to present and calculate the outcomes of compound statements is to use a <truth table - these might look familiar from computer science, where the associated algebra is known as Boolean algebra and the T/F is replaced with 1/0.

Laws of the Algebra of Propositions¶

There are identity laws, i.e. using AND or OR like multiplying or adding on 1 or 0:

\[\begin{split}p \vee F &= p \\ p \wedge F &= F \\ p \wedge T &= p \\ p \vee T &= T\end{split}\]

Associative laws, i.e. does the position of the brackets matter:

\[\begin{split}(p \vee q) \vee r &= p \vee (q \vee r)\\ (p \wedge q) \wedge r &= p \wedge (q \wedge r)\end{split}\]

Commutative laws, i.e. does the order of operations matter:

\[\begin{split}p \vee q &= q \vee p \\ p \wedge q &= q \wedge p\end{split}\]

Distributive laws, i.e. does the operation in a bracket expand out:

\[\begin{split}p \vee (q \wedge r) &= (p\vee q) \wedge (p \wedge r)\\ p \wedge (q \vee r) &= (p\wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)\end{split}\]

Involution laws, i.e. is the inverse of the inverse return to the original state:

\[\neg(\neg p) = p\]

Idempotent laws, i.e. it’s everything or nothing:

\[\begin{split}p \vee p &= p \\ p \wedge p &= p\end{split}\]

Complement laws, i.e. does everything add up together or subtract to zero:

\[\begin{split}p \vee \neg p &= T \\ p \wedge \neg p &= F \\ \neg T &= F \\ \neg F &= T\end{split}\]

And finally DeMorgan’s laws, which refer to overall inverse relations:

\[\begin{split}\neg(p \vee q) &= \neg p \wedge \neg q \\ \neg(p \wedge q) &= \neg p \vee \neg q\end{split}\]

which we see mirrors the algebra of sets.

Arguments and Fallacies¶

A logical argument is a situation where there are given set of propositions \(\{P_1,\,P_2,\,\dots P_n\}\) called Premises have that as a consequence (or yield) a proposition \(Q\) known as a Conclusion, we denote this:

\[\{P_1,\,P_2,\,\dots P_n \}\vdash Q\]

An argument \(\{P_1,\,P_2,\,\dots P_n\} \vdash Q\) is said to be valid if \(Q\) is true whenever all the premises \(\{P_1,\,P_2,\,\dots P_n\}\) are true but otherwise it is false. An argument that is not true is known as a fallacy.

Logical Implications¶

\[P(p,\,q,\,\dots) \Rightarrow Q(p,\,q,\,\dots) \]
